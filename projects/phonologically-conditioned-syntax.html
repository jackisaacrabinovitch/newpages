Phonologically Conditioned Syntax

Phonologically conditioned syntax is a phenomenon whereby the word order of a sentence is dictated or influenced by the phonology of the the sentences component parts (or the phonology of would-be alternatives to said sentence). Such a topic is rich with potential work, but I have particularly carved out a niche in looking at how we can use the general phenomenon of phonologically conditioned syntax to make testable predictions about sound change in corpora which is typically thought to be phonologically opaque, such as Chinese.

Although how tonogenesis happened between Old and Middle Chinese is roughly known, the exact time of tonogenesis is as of yet unclear. By searching Classical Chinese texts for bigrams which would have violated certain OCP constraints at different points in tonogenesis, I have been attempting to more accurately represent when and in what manner tonogenesis came about in Chinese, based on the assumption that marked sound structures may have influenced authorsâ€™ word choice and word order, thus leading them to avoid OCP violations in their texts.
